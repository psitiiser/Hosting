Physics of Finger-snapping



-[Author Name]

Most research labs have interesting ways to recover from a session of intense technical discussion. Saad Bhamla’s lab at Georgia Tech is no exception. Two years ago, after one typically intense scientific presentation by one of the students, their chilling session nicknamed “Super Happy Fun Time” settled at talking about the Avengers: Infinity War movie.

Considering how Bhamla’s lab works on ultrafast motion in nature, it was probably inevitable that the talk turned to Thanos’ finger snap while wearing a metal glove. Bhamla said, “I’m willing to make a bet that if you had metallic gauntlets like Thanos has, I would suspect that it’s actually very difficult to store energy in a controllable way.”

Energy has to be stored between the fingers in a controllable way so that it gets quickly released and you get a perfect snap. This led Raghav Acharya, one of the students, to set up an experiment to study finger-snapping. He first got the action on video by putting some reflective dots on his fingers so he could automatically track the finger movement when taking a side view video with a high-speed camera.

This video helped them to figure out the velocity and acceleration involved: a snap takes 7 milliseconds, which when compared to the apparent lifetime of 150 milliseconds an eye-blink takes, is very impressive. Even more impressive is its acceleration that can even beat the throwing speed of a baseball pitcher by three times. What was it that allowed a completely ordinary human being to perform such a seemingly extraordinary feat?

Bhamla's lab played around with friction to test how it affected the speed of snapping. If you've ever tried snapping with wet fingers, you would know the resulting snaps won't be as…well, snappy. The research team confirmed this too, and so counterintuitively figured that maybe higher friction would do the trick. However, this didn't work either. When you increase the friction, say, by putting on some high-friction rubber pads, you would waste too much energy trying to get the fingers to slide against each other.

As Bhamla put it: “So it turns out that, in our experiments, we find that the skin friction is kind of this optimal sweet spot in this Goldilocks zone that gives you enough energy but also detaches quickly to give you the snap [snaps].”

Acharya concludes in his paper, “Loading and unlatching are both heavily mediated by the latch friction, which makes this an excellent example of a friction-based LaMSA system.” Here, LaMSA refers to Latch Mediated Spring Actuated Systems.

With these results, they came to the conclusion that Thanos with his metal gauntlet would have had quite a weak and unsatisfactory snap. What the lab is even more curious about right now is whether other animals too can snap fingers and how satisfactorily they can do this!
